    Many people are worried about the prospect of a clone being born in the name of science. Although we have just heard of it, creating humans by cloning is like a blasphemous act, going against morals and ethical conceptions from Asia to Europe, experts say that biologically, there are can create a human-like version of the real person without having to carry the burden of labor. However, even if the ethical issue is put aside, the internal resources used to create a real person are still a difficult barrier to overcome.

    Since the 1950s, when researchers successfully cloned a frog, since then experts have "produced" dozens of animals, including mice, cats, sheep, pigs, and cows. In each case, they always face problems that need to be overcome, according to Fox News, Dr. Robert Lanza of Advanced Cell Technology, which works in the field of cell therapy to treat diseases. for humans and animal cloning. With mice, researchers can use thousands of eggs and run many experiments to find ways to handle errors in the implementation. "It's like a counting game," says Dr. Lanza. However, in the case of primates, eggs are a very rare and not easily available resource for experiments. Besides, they couldn't simply apply what they learned by cloning mice or cows to humans. For example, cloning animals requires researchers to first remove the nucleus of an egg cell. In doing this, they also removed all the proteins needed to support cell division. This was not a problem in the mice, as the ovum produced was still able to produce these proteins again. However, primates can't do that, and researchers say this is one of the reasons why attempts to clone monkeys have failed. And yet, cloning animals often leads to all sorts of genetic abnormalities, preventing implantation of the ovum in the uterus, or causing embryos to be rejected, or test subjects to die shortly after birth. These abnormalities are quite common when cloning animals because embryos are implanted with only one parent instead of both as in the case of natural conception. This means that the molecular process is known as “genomic imprinting” does not take place in the correct sequence in cloned embryos. Having trouble at this stage can lead to the placenta being many times larger than usual, hindering the blood circulation to the fetus. In one experiment, Dr. Lanza and colleagues cloned a species of cattle called a banteng bison. And the animal was born at twice the normal size, forcing the team of experts to perform euthanasia for it. In addition to the usual moral problems, the extremely high mortality rate and the risk of developing deformities further expose the work of cloning to face fierce opposition from society. “It's like putting your baby on a rocket and knowing that the probability of exploding is 50-50. Downright unethical,” concludes Dr. Lanza.
